Assignment 2: Critical Reading Reflection — Mobile-Optimised Guide

📌 OVERVIEW & PURPOSE

This assignment is designed to develop your critical thinking, analytical reading, and academic writing skills. You are required to select one of the key themes identified from our class survey, choose two provided academic articles related to that theme, and write a critical reflection that analyses and synthesises the arguments presented in those texts.

Your response should not be a simple summary but a critical engagement with the ideas, arguments, and evidence presented by the authors.

🎯 THEMES AND TOPICS

You MUST choose ONE of the following seven themes:

  • Theme 1: Unemployment – Youth unemployment, labour-market scarring, skills mismatch, economic policies affecting job creation.
  • Theme 2: Corruption – Systemic corruption, its impact on economic development and public trust, ethics in leadership and reporting, case studies (e.g., state capture).
  • Theme 3: Entrepreneurship – Entrepreneurship as a solution to unemployment, barriers to entry, access to capital, financial literacy, innovation in the South African context.
  • Theme 4: National Dialogue & Governance – Service delivery, public policy effectiveness, constitutionalism, the role of national dialogue in democracy, governance and accountability.
  • Theme 5: Education and Skills Mismatch – Alignment between education and the labour market, financial literacy, the "failures" of the education system, solutions for skills development.
  • Theme 6: Economic Policies & Issues – Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment (B-BBEE), National Health Insurance (NHI), inflation, repo rates, trade tariffs, wealth inequality.
  • Theme 7: Social Justice & Inequality – Crime, poverty, access to opportunities, human rights, social cohesion, and their relationship with economic factors.

A folder titled "Reading Reflection Articles" is available on clickUP. Inside, you will find seven sub-folders — one for each theme. Each contains exactly TWO academic articles.

✍️ WRITING YOUR REFLECTION

Your 850–1000 word reflection must address these four sections:

1. Introduction

Begin by stating the authors' names and their central claims regarding your chosen theme. Do not begin with “I believe…” or “In my view…”. Instead, write: “In their respective analyses, Smith (2022) argues that youth unemployment is structurally entrenched due to skills mismatch, while Lee (2023) contends that policy inertia, not skill gaps, is the primary barrier.” Then present your thesis: how these arguments interact — agree, conflict, complement — and what your critical perspective reveals.

2. Summary and Analysis

Succinctly summarise the core argument of EACH article separately. For each:

  • What is the author’s main claim?
  • What evidence (data, case studies, theory) do they use?
  • How does their approach relate to the South African context?

Each article must be summarised individually before comparison.

3. Synthesis and Critical Engagement

This is the most important section. Compare and contrast the two articles. Do the authors agree or disagree? How do their arguments complement or challenge each other? Critically evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of their evidence and reasoning. Connect their findings directly to broader socio-economic realities in South Africa.

4. Conclusion

Summarise your key insights. What does this comparative analysis reveal about your chosen theme? End with a thoughtful, evidence-based closing statement — not a personal opinion.

📄 FORMATTING & SUBMISSION REQUIREMENTS

  • File Format: Submit a single document in .docx or .pdf format.
  • Font: Times New Roman or Arial — size ≤ 12pt
  • Spacing: 1.5
  • Referencing: Harvard style ONLY
  • Each of the TWO chosen articles must be cited at least twice in-text.
  • All statistics, percentages, figures — must include page numbers.
  • A complete Reference List must appear at the end with full bibliographic details.

✅ Referencing Examples (Harvard)

In-text citation:
“Labour-market scarring reduces lifetime earnings by up to 22%” (Mokoena, 2021, p. 7);
“Entrepreneurship training fails to address structural exclusion” (Nkosi & Dlamini, 2022, p. 14).

Reference List entry:
Mokoena, T., 2021. ‘Youth unemployment and long-term wage effects’, South African Journal of Economics, 89(3), pp. 5–22.
Nkosi, L. and Dlamini, R., 2022. ‘Skills mismatch in higher education: A critique of policy narratives’, Journal of Higher Education in Africa, 20(1), pp. 10–28.
